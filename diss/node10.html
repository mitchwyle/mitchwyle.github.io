Information Retrieval, Homeosemy, Natural Language

             
By eliminating time and space as barriers, the very achievements that put us within reach of a truly global economy are burying us in irrelevant and useless data, in mountains of low-quality ore that must be laboriously refined for relevant information, the signal hidden in the noise. The critical resource for turning this raw data into useful information is computer software, as strategic a resource in the information age as petroleum is today. -- B. Cox, There Is a Silver Bullet, Byte, October 1990, p. 209.

We cannot give a comprehensive overview of IR  in this thesis; the reader is instead referred to some standard texts [Salt83]   and [Rijs79]   as well as a newer and more formal dense tutorial of IR concepts [Scha92] .

Information retrieval   systems  exist to satisfy the information  needs of their users. Although some newer browsing  methods [Lesk91] allow for searching without a query, traditional IR always assumes that the information need of the user is modeled as a query  text. The well-known distinction between user-relevance  and  query-relevance, i.e. the difference between how well a document satisfies the information need of the user versus how well it matches a query, is one of the fundamental concerns both in formulating definitions of relevance  and also in new proposals for measuring retrieval performance  (see section 2.4). The important distinction between queries in standard  test  collections whose supposed absolute , universal     relevance is determined by a committee, and queries whose relevance is expressed by an individual is discussed only informally, even in some modern studies of relevance [Lose91]. Additionally, the obvious time-dependence   of an individual's information need, though mentioned occasionally [Swan88], [Clev91], has never been modeled or directly taken into account. In an interactive system, it is plausible that two individual documents which are quite low on a user's ranked preference list, suddenly shoot up the list when retrieved and viewed together. Preferences among a retrieval set of documents can therefore change suddenly during a single interactive session; individual, absolute relevance assessments are therefore an inadequate model for user     relevance judgements. User-preferences are probably better. In a subscription service, changes in relevance over longer time intervals are the result of many factors, not just the effects of viewing documents from the service.

The field of IR has boiled down to the mechanics of finding messages that are most similar  to a query text. The similarity  in meaning  of two texts is sometimes called homeosemy  [Karl77]. This similarity is important not only for retrieving documents relevant to a query, but also for clustering  similar documents together for storage and also for building information  structures to categorize  the messages. These structures can often assist in the browsing and retrieval processes of large collections  [Salt91].

In the next chapter we shall offer different and, we hope, slightly better alternatives to some common IR assumptions; but let us first consider the normal IR paradigms  and the body of knowledge gleaned through the last four decades of scientific investigations into text  analysis and retrieval . Perhaps the most important common result from the large number of text retrieval studies through the years is that a detailed and complex semantic  analysis of all messages in a collection is often not practical, necessary, or even effective in approximating homeosemy for the purpose of deriving good retrieval performance results. Instead, the best results for very large collections are obtained by systems which rely upon the statistics  of term occurrences    in texts to approximate similarities. This statistical approximation of homeosemy is not only computationally very practical, but also yields surprisingly good retrieval performance when compared to many methods of manual indexing . We therefore build upon these practical and effective methods, instead of proposing yet another radical new model for IR.

In addition to relevance  feedback and other traditional methods  of improving  a query text, we shall also explore aspects of a user's information  need that are not encoded in a query text. These aspects include time-dependence, new resource discovery  , retrieval set size, and very specific sets of messages to be included or eliminated from a retrieval set, independent of the ranking of the messages. This exploration takes us into a further discussion of new definitions of relevance  and effectiveness, as well as an analysis and clarification of communication and ``information need.''  

There is some indication that methods for   natural language processing  have finally matured to the point that they can be used effectively for retrieval and filtering [Ande92], [Hays91], [Rau91], [Jaco92], [Jac92a]. However, other recent studies still point out the extreme rigidity and major shortcomings inherent in these models [Maul91], [Salt91]. Although our methods do incorporate some simple semantic  components such as stop-lists, word reduction, and phrases, we do not pursue advanced natural language parsing  (NLP) methods in this work.
