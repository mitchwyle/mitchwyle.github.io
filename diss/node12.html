Information Overload

     

The amplified connectivity  to other individuals and retrieval sources associated specifically with computer mediated communication  systems  but also the Matrix (of inter-connected wide area networks and other communication systems) in general leads to the reception of too much unwanted information, a now-common phenomenon called information overload [Hilt85], [Quar90]. This phenomenon was actually encountered quite early in the history  of the Matrix [Hilt77], but affected relatively few individuals. Overload itself and it's deleterious effects have been compared to living under extremely crowded conditions; corresponding solutions to the latter problem have been found to be appropriate for solving the former. The literature [Hilt78], [Hilt85] also suggests a few automated methods for overcoming electronic information overload, but most of these methods are echoes of the same mistakes made repeatedly in the field of IR over the last 35 years. These popular mistakes  include:

  1. that a static semantic  network or semantic hierarchy  which is artificially imposed on a system with a highly dynamic collection will magically allow all users of the system to navigate the message collection and find all and only those messages for which they are searching,
  2. that Boolean  search  methods are the perfect way to select messages of interest,
  3. that indexing, searching, retrieval, and dissemination methods which are developed for a few dozen messages will function perfectly well for much larger collections,
  4. that precision and statistically estimated recall are unrelated and a very good way to measure the effectiveness of a system, and
  5. that ``artificial intelligence''  (whatever such a phrase should mean) will magically cure all problems of such systems in the very near future.
Hiltz and Turoff [Hilt85] vaguely suggest semantic  hierarchical organizations, and some ``ordering primitives  '' based on the projected audience of a WAN message. They also suggest that users develop the skill of skimming  messages in order to increase recall. These suggestions are hauntingly reminiscent of early failures to improve IR performance [Clev91]. Cleverdon  encountered very similar sentiments during the early Cranfield   test collection studies; specifically, the Western Reserve  University's Metallurgical Index used a semantic indexing scheme on a very small collection and failed to improve retrieval performance. We believe that a more productive way to combat information overload is through the application and refinement of modern IR methods incorporated in an automatic message filtering system.

In this chapter, we examined some of the problems and issues involved in information dissemination, defining some terms and describing specific aspects of the current state-of-the-art in computer networks, information retrieval, relevance theory, and electronic information overload. We have, however, not yet presented the tools to formalize retrieval and dissemination models. In the next chapter, we shall examine methods for modelling the Matrix and effectively extracting messages from it. We also set forth the IR framework  for the key components of new text comparison algorithms. Finally, we shall review a new measurement of the relevance of a communicated message based on user preference instead of absolute pertinence  .   
