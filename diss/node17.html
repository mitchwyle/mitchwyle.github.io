Efficacy

       

...it often happens that people agree that two things are both useful but do not agree about which is the more so... -- Aristotle Rhetoric I (7) 5.

Precision - recall   graphs   from standardized test collections are used pervasively in the IR literature to evaluate the performance of retrieval systems. These measures are simple, intuitive and straightforward. Researchers over the years have become comfortable comparing retrieval performance using these measures, but many subtle dangers arise when using them [Fre91a]. An important shortcoming of these measures is their sensitivity to outlying values in an experimental environment. When too little data are used, there is no indication of the error associated with their calculation.

Precision-oriented users  are   interested in how many non-relevant or  ``fallout'' messages they will have to view for a given retrieval set size. Recall-oriented users usually prefer to ascertain system performance by counting how large a retrieval set size they must view in order to obtain a certain number of relevant messages. This subtle but    important effectiveness consideration is hidden by the curve-smoothing process used to construct precision - recall graphs [Fre91a]. A number of other effectiveness measures that are simple variations of precision - recall have been proposed to make up for some of these shortcomings [Salt83]. However, they all use binary relevance assessments. That is, these effectiveness evaluation methods all use the absolute  relevant /   non-relevant judgement as their cornerstone.

As we pointed out above, the key deficiency is the use of absolute, direct judgement instead of preferences. As many, repeatable experiments have pointed out [Frei91], the notion of preference is much more powerful and useful in the evaluation of information systems.

SDI  services model the user's information  need  with a so-called user profile.    One of the most important aspects of any SDI system is how well a profile accurately and effectively mirrors its user's information need. Commercial SDI  services  use   a simple Boolean   retrieval model and stored Boolean queries for user profiles. Delivered items are usually not ranked, and when the effectiveness of such systems is measured, the measurements always rely on statistics over binary relevance assessments.

The usefulness of a retrieved item depends not only on its timeliness  and availability , but also on the user's momentary interest    and preferences. It is also intuitively evident that the relevance and usefulness of individual items in a rapidly changing source message collection must be different from day to day. Although definitions of relevance in information retrieval are once again gaining attention in the literature [Lose91], measurements other than precision - recall have unfortunately not been heavily exploited for performance comparisons in evaluating new IR systems.

Usefulness [Frei91] is an effectiveness measure which compares two retrieval algorithms; given two algorithms, A and B, expresses whether A is more effective than B or vice versa. A positive value indicates that B is more effective than A; a negative value signifies that B is less effective than A. The measure is specifically suited to measure IR algorithm performance in a WAN environment. The notion of item relevance in this effectiveness measure does not have to be an absolute binary (relevant / non-relevant) judgement. Instead, subscribers may rank information relevance relative to other items. The viability of usefulness was demonstrated in [Wyle91] and its advantages to traditional measures are evident in [Fre91a]. The subscription service implementation described in chapter four employs the usefulness  measure  to evaluate algorithm performance .

If t is less than 0, i.e. one considers past preferences, it becomes possible to model partially the change in a person's preferences over time. In such a situation, the person would be tasked with specifying preferences for a given set of messages at two or more separate times, and an analysis could be performed of any differences in the two sets of preferences. For our purposes, however, we are not interested in the change in user preferences over time, but rather in predicting    a preference at some future time.

As we shall see in detail in the next chapter, our requirement for a temporal  preference  is related to new resource  discovery . In general, when new information items become available for retrieval, no preferences for these messages are available for determining whether or not these new items should be viewed or not. In fact, SDI retrieval systems, based on stored subscriber profiles and retrieval algorithms, form system preferences of these new items. Evaluation of the retrieval performance of such systems is then based on comparing these system preferences to those supplied at some later time by a subscriber. When one considers the time dimension of this process, it is apparent that an SDI system is really trying to predict subscriber-preferences of newly arriving messages.

The question then arises, why even try formally to model the future relative relevance judgements of some person? As we alluded earlier, the answer lies in the specification of a filtering stage which is designed to find new sources of information which would be preferred to other sources.

According to [Scha89], these preference relations induce a perfect ranking of equivalence classes. Because the tuple set is defined as exhaustive, the equivalence classes contain individual messages. This ranking in turn yields a numbering ([Scha89], p. 18) which is identical to the rankings resulting from the RSV functions defined in section 2.2. Using a subscriber-defined cutoff point c, we can now define the ``optimal'' set of messages (i.e. the set containing all and only those messages which the subscriber prefers to all others in the Matrix at time t) to be the set containing the c highest ranked messages induced by the exhaustive set of conjectural preference relations . As we shall see in the next chapter, an analysis involving this set can be used to define partially a subscriber's future information need. Such a model can then be used to construct specific filtering stages aimed at optimizing retrieval performance by targeting specific qualities of this optimal set in their definition. This set can also form the basis for methods aimed at assisting subscribers at new resource discovery.

In this chapter we defined a new model for querying the Matrix, and established an advanced feature weighting function to be incorporated in vector space ranking algorithms based on paragraph types in WAN messages. We also examined some effectiveness matters, concluding that the usefulness measure is best suited for analyzing the effectiveness of WAN-based retrieval systems or any system which has a large or highly dynamic message collection. Finally, we introduced the notion of temporal preferences, including conjectural preferences which approximate a future preference. We come now to the heart of this work, namely our approach to filtering the Matrix.
