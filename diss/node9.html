Agents in the Matrix

   
Knowledge is of two kinds; we know a subject ourselves, or we know where we can find information upon it. -- S. Johnson, Letter to Lord Chesterfield, February, 1755.

Advances in computer hardware and telecommunications technology have spawned an exponential growth of the Matrix [Quar90], a worldwide meta-network  of connected computer networks  and conferencing   systems; the Matrix provides unique services that are similar to but more powerful than those of the telephone , post office, library or telefax . The Matrix consists of WANs; each WAN  links thousands (or hundreds of thousands) of computers together and provides a distributed computer-mediated communications  system to millions of users. Information is delivered quickly over large geographical distances; information sources for IR are no longer limited to a single machine, a local area network (LAN ), a metropolitan area network (MAN ), or even a single wide-area network (WAN).

Communities  of individuals congeal around particular WANs and topics of discussion supported by the Matrix. Preexisting communities use this medium to further their goals. New applications  of the Matrix include tasks that could ordinarily not be completed in time to be useful without computer networks. These tasks  include:

  • The coordination  of large software projects with many programmers or individuals separated by large geographical distances
  • Data transfer   for coordination of astronomical  observations
  • Medical data  transfer 
  • News  of political  situations
  • The production   of scholarly reports  and books 
  • Scientific collaboration  in Biology , Chemistry , Physics 

As the Matrix expands, each of these application areas requires better methods to access  and filter the information  flood. The ultimate application of this thesis is to provide a foundation  for developing and using such methods.

Alan Kay  has informally   proposed ``semi-intelligent software programs that you can delegate your goals to that will act like your personal news reporters, whose jobs will be to scour the networks that you are connected to and find things that are relevant to the goals you are pursuing right now. their job is not just to find anecdotes but also tools and other kinds of things that are related to what you are doing'' [Kay91]. Related ideas are now appearing in diverse areas of computer science literature (for a survey, see [Seel91]). SDI software systems could be considered a kind of agent, but most existing SDI systems have a human intermediary  or organization involved in their use. Furthermore, these systems do not help their subscribers with the discovery  of other information  resources. Commercial  SDI services obviously do not want to encourage their subscribers to use competing services, and public systems (for example in libraries) are not designed to assist resource discovery [Maur74]. Another class of software systems which may loosely be considered ``agents'' are systems which store and execute a list of commands on behalf of the user at specified times. Such systems exist in most computing environments, and are typically used in conjunction with non-interactive software . Employing such systems in conjunction with a sophisticated ``user-emulation '' or scripting  system  would approximate Kay's concept more closely. One can conclude that agents, in general, are still in a developmental stage; it is probable that they will become more prominent in the future .

We shall now review the major software designs for retrieving information from the Matrix. The most notable WAN-based information  server models include the WAIS  project, [Kahl91], the internet gopher  [Albe91], the information lens   [Malo87], the distributed  indexing model [Danz91], Xanadu  [Nels88], and the polychannel  system  [Giff90].

WAIS is an IR system developed by Thinking  Machines Inc. for searching inverted  file text databases available over a WAN. It is based on a two-tiered search  strategy whereby a user first queries a directory  of database servers for choosing appropriate source  databases, and then queries the databases  themselves for messages. The directory of servers can be a WAN-distributed database [Ever90]. In the baseline WAIS implementation, the directory of servers is centrally located at one node on in the Matrix. However, the domain-name server (DNS) [Ever90] distributed database system has been grafted into WAIS to solve the   single-point-of-failure and access  problems of a central  directory. The network protocol , user-interface, and retrieval algorithm in WAIS can use relevance   feedback data to incorporate terms from relevant documents into the query. In the WAIS model, the directory of servers does not contain much information about the source databases themselves and is not updated when source collections change. This drawback may be a fatal flaw in the WAIS architecture [Giff90]. The original design concept of the system [Kahl89] calls for an SDI component, called ``dynamic folders  ,'' in which queries would automatically, periodically be executed. In this ``dynamic folders'' proposal, a special file directory has an agent associated with it. The state information of the agent is called a ``charter '' and consists of a Boolean database query, a list of asynchronous event types and a time interval for re-running the query as well as some user-interface semantics. No viable realization of this concept has emerged from the WAIS project, however.

The internet gopher   is similar to WAIS, but is based on a much simpler client-server protocol; it provides a distributed hierarchy  of full-text messages from several host machines in the Matrix. The client part can either browse  the directory hierarchy and documents of several servers, or issue a Boolean query to a single server. Such queries range over all documents contained in that server. The goal of the gopher project is to present users of a  campus-wide information  system with a single, network-transparent, unified view  of the information resources on the campus; the metaphor  is a local computer file-system and file searching software. There is no SDI component in the internet gopher.

Malone's information   lens is an email user agent  (UA ) with filtering and mailing list  capabilities. Such features are quite common in email user agents [Wyl89a]. A unique feature of the information lens is the email alias ``anyone '' to which users can send a message which will then be readable by all users in an organization. Users specify filtering rules by filling in forms  or templates , which can retrieve messages sent to ``anyone.'' The email filtering functions include the usual sorting, pre-filing, and deletion. The information lens user-interface incorporates a concept similar to our palette of examples (see section 3.1) user-interface dialog whereby the system presents menus  for the construction of filtering  rules  which help guide users through the creation  of new rules. We take this concept a step further by furnishing complete examples instead of a myriad of menu choices.

The distributed   indexing model is a vague proposal for a distributed database and retrieval system to be used by information providers on the Matrix. It prescribes a complex, hierarchical , cooperating network  protocol instead of the now standard client-server paradigm. The advantage of this model is its scalability ; the authors claim it can easily handle network nodes. One major disadvantage of the proposal is the assumption that all Matrix resource providers can and will cooperate with ``index brokers '' (the postulated intermediate  systems); another drawback is the large amount of data redundancy   and replicated storage  overhead  it requires to cache  the requests and data. This system also appears to be in an early conceptual phase of development, as no implementations have been reported.

The Xanadu  project is hypertext inventor Ted  Nelson's ambitious attempt to create a system providing the simultaneous connection and access of all published text in the world via hypertext  links  which span the Matrix. A new file system  is proposed with network  transparent hypertext  links  embedded in its design. Although a few Xanadu booths  have been installed in one U.S. city, the concept has had neither commercial success nor widespread academic acceptance. Other network transparent hypermedia systems have been proposed (a broad survey can be found in [Micha91]; retrieval models for large hypermedia webs are described in [Fre91c]) . IR based on hypermedia  webs have taken directions outside of Nelson's original doctrine. Xanadu includes no SDI provisions and retrieval itself is based solely on interactive browsing .

Gifford's  Polychannel  model  is based on both broadcast  (simplex ) media   such as radio  or cable  TV in a metropolitan area network  (MAN ) and traditional (duplex ) digital communications media in the Matrix. Subscribers receive a constant stream  of information  from the simplex medium and actively store only information matching a local filtering  specification. Subscribers can formulate queries via the duplex communications medium in order to retrieve messages which do not match their local filters . The system is based on simple text matching and includes no IR ranking  or similarity measures.
