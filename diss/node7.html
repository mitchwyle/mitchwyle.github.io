Background

   
Most of the things about which we make decisions, and into which therefore we inquire, present us with alternative possibilities. For it is about our actions that we deliberate and inquire. -- Aristotle,   Rhetoric I (2) 24.

The problems associated with the communication and retrieval of recorded information have been under investigation in one way or another since the earliest recorded times in western civilization. [Swan88]. The interrelated phenomena of information storage, communication, access, indexing, reduction, overload, assessment, and even the theory and definition of information itself all play important rôles in diverse solutions to the same fundamental problem of effectively filtering and disseminating information. The issues involved are at the same time complex and simple, solvable, and untenable, broad and specific. In this dissertation, we propose new methods to address this age-old exigency, integrating the use of wide area network (WAN) communication with modern information retrieval (IR) algorithms. The viability of our methods is demonstrated in an implementation of a subscription service called Pasadena ( Periodic, asynchronous selective extr action and diss emination of information through network access) which provides a new type of solution to the ancient IR problem.

Vannevar Bush's  paper ``As We May Think''  [Bush45] is usually taken as the starting point for the intensive research in information science which lead to the creation and explosive development of such fields as hypertext  , databases  [Smit91], and IR . (Condensed histories of the creation, evolution, and citation patterns in these fields appear in [Swan88], and [Smit81].) However, much thought and effort was dedicated to the improvement of information systems for millennia before Bush's postulated memex machine. In fact, Aristotle, Plato , and Cicero  produced important insights into these issues long before computers came along. Bush's memex and the subsequent developments in computer science have concentrated almost all modern efforts on the development of automatic indexing  and passive  retrieval systems. The problem of reading information overload , and the blind application of simple, automatic computer solutions to complex IR problems have given rise to a generation of software which addresses neither user requirements  nor the fundamental underlying information dissemination  problems. Priority has been given to ``information management''   instead of information filtering, analysis or promulgation  .

Another contributing factor to the dilemma is an unfortunate deficiency in the analysis of the influence that communicated information has on recipients. More specifically, there is a scarcity of useful operational methods which systematically measure user-satisfaction   and effectiveness  of information communication . Investigations into the motivation and purpose for information communication have been neglected; even simple measurements of information relevance and preference are used inconsistently or informally in the literature. It is well known that assessing information value  cannot be measured objectively. There has therefore been a subtle shift of emphasis away from information significance towards ``data'' manipulation , which causes the worth  and effectiveness  of ideas, notions or views to be concepts for which categorization  and judgement  are avoided. As a direct result of this intentional avoidance, issues such as the motivation  for and success  of communicated messages are ignored. Information filtering based on the immediate value  of information becomes almost impossible under such conditions. There is weighty evidence in the literature [Bern79] [Scha90] that these difficult issues must be addressed in order to implement better solutions to the IR problem. Bernier  and Yerkey  spend three chapters outlining the ``cultural disasters''  resulting from the lack of cogency  in information science. Schamber  et. al. argue for radically new definitions of relevance  which incorporate rhetorical qualities of communication  in order to improve IR models.

Part of the purpose of this dissertation, therefore, is the consideration of communication effectiveness  as this issue relates to information filtering  and dissemination. Recently, in the academic discipline of IR, a general consensus has formed that there is an urgent need for more and better relevance measurements  to improve the overall effectiveness of IR systems [Frei91] . We shall present the application of a new model of retrieval performance measurement in our development of better indexing and retrieval algorithms for selective dissemination of information (SDI) systems based on messages in WANs.

From our discussion thus far, we beg the questions, ``what do current approaches miss in their considerations of communication  effectiveness?'' and ``what tangible benefits arise for real systems by re-examining these issues?'' Analyzing and incorporating effectiveness considerations into the design of new models for information systems is essential to their success at delivering all and only that information needed (or preferred) by the individuals who use them. Our argument is that most existing models, because they are based on an inappropriate retrieval performance evaluation paradigm, are not effective at solving the underlying problems for which they are constructed, namely predicting and delivering that information which is preferred to all other available information. We contend that part of the performance problem in IR is due to the inappropriate, prevailing methods for measuring retrieval performance. The benefits that can be attained by incorporating an analysis and measurement of user-preference  into new IR- and user-models are as follows: First and foremost, a system can be better optimized for retrieval performance if the performance is measured in terms of criteria which are closer to the real motive for the system's development (sending messages which a receiver prefers). Secondly, the empirical evidence gathered is more easily reproducible when effectiveness measures  based on preferences are used instead of measures based on absolute relevance judgements . Finally, the method we employ includes an error probability calculation which is very helpful in experimental evaluation of algorithm performance .

Current approaches to solving IR problems fall into two paradigms, active search and selective dissemination of information (SDI).    In the former model, a user is supposed to search in several different source databases for messages which are ``relevant'' to the user's momentary information need . Systems based on the latter paradigm use very simple (and therefore lower retrieval performance) methods for comparing a query text to individual messages. As we shall see in section 3.2, our approach is to combine modern IR searching  methods (including new refinements which we developed) with a more sophisticated selective dissemination of information (SDI) model in order to enhance the effectiveness of a subscription service . The methods we shall present in the next two chapters are beneficial both to information service providers     (information brokers) and to information consumers   (subscribers). Subscribers gain advantages through using these methods because of the flexibility and increased retrieval performance they provide. An information provider gains two distinct advantages by using our models instead of existing approaches. First of all, useless and redundant information  sources  are automatically eliminated, producing a drastic reduction in storage requirements  for the information service provider. Secondly, computing and network resources   can be minimized by optimizing the configuration of separate filtering stages (discussed in chapter 3). Tangible benefits of the models we are about to present include increased efficiency   for networked SDI systems, more automation of the SDI process (a savings in tedious labour), and significantly increased retrieval performance for subscribers. These benefits can be realized directly in the construction of new electronic subscription services. A prototype example of such a service is presented in chapter 4.

A reconsideration of the ancient issue of rhetoric and the specific matter of cogency is necessary not only for the useful definition of the key concept of relevance, but also for defining and measuring retrieval performance and user-satisfaction of retrieval systems. In the next chapter we shall briefly sketch out a simple way of measuring cogency and strategies for increasing system performance based on the measure.

In this thesis, we shall introduce the concept of filtering in stages   , and characterize specific properties for their effective construction and application. We also discuss certain dependencies  among these stages. A new term weighting scheme is applied in one filtering stage which produces significant retrieval performance improvements. A new phrase generation technique is also developed for creating better indexing features from queries and messages. We also introduce a novel user-model which forms the basis of the design and prototype implementation of an electronic information service. This system solves the specific heretofore unsolved problem of finding the preferred WAN messages among collections with ( to ) messages. The passive user model provides a possible solution to the still unresolved problem of making complicated but powerful features of sophisticated information systems available to more users, including novices. The concept of separating distinct filtering stages out of the overall retrieval and dissemination process adds more flexibility to existing solutions to the IR problem. The decomposition  also creates the possibility of running certain stages in parallel  on separate processors and at different parts of a WAN. As we shall see in section 3.2, this flexibility allows for a much more efficient use of the network bandwidth   and processors; it therefore allows for certain unsolved problems arising from bandwidth or storage  limitations to be solved. The most important solution made possible through the new methods we shall present is in the struggle against reading  information overload. Our filtering methods reduce data volume  to the exact level specified by an individual.
