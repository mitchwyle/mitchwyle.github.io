Performance Evaluation in an Information Server

One of the fundamental problems with the vast proliferation of computer systems and computer networks is the effective dissemination and retrieval of newly available dynamic information. Because of its exponential growth, the WAN information base within the Matrix has become more difficult to query and use. On the other hand, electronic mail and user-friendly client - server network software have established themselves as well-defined mechanisms for communicating between people and computer systems both within an organization and over a WAN between machines thousands of kilometers distant. With this growing connectivity, it is a natural idea to couple the WAN information sources to individual WAN users through an SDI subscription service. The key element of such an information server (IS) is its ability effectively to filter the WAN-available information for its subscribers.

However, it is impossible to measure this filtering ability in a real-world situation using the classical effectiveness measures developed for static documents and small standard test collections. Hence, we applied the usefulness effectiveness measure, which is particularly suited to the dynamic message collections found in a subscription service. The choice of effectiveness measure can be one of the most important aspects in information retrieval science because it provides for the accurate, empirical measurement of retrieval and filtering methods in a real-world environment. The usefulness measure is very well suited to gauge algorithm performance in the dynamic environment of the Matrix. The measure uses all preference relations contained in each subscriber's ranking of a retrieved set of messages. The notion of relevance in this effectiveness measure is not an absolute binary (relevant / non-relevant) judgement. Instead, subscribers rank information relevance relative to other items. The usefulness measure also includes an error probability calculation that is very helpful in experimental evaluation of algorithm performance. The error calculation indicates the reliability of the measured difference in performance between two algorithms, and therefore provides a stopping criterion for on-going experiments.

The objectives of the theoretical model and implementation of this information server project are:

  • to test the viability of the concept of filtering in stages in a large-scale realistic environment,
  • to provide a testbed for the iterative development of high-performance vector-space retrieval algorithms, and secondarily,
  • to provide a useful service to subscribers.

The general framework of filtering in stages is not only more flexible for the implementor of an IS, but also advantageous to subscribers using such systems. Each of the filtering stages described in section 3.2 is targeted at a single, well-defined aspect of the subscriber's information need. This specificity provides a clearer, more direct method for a subscriber to express his information need. It also entails more tractable and direct methods for a subscriber to make changes to his profile to keep it more isomorphic with his actual information need as covered by the highly dynamic Matrix. Profiles within our IS model include a mechanism to help subscribers discover new Matrix resources as they become available. This aspect of a subscriber's information need is modeled formally by the notion of a conjectural preference.

Experiments run within the Pasadena IS give empirical evidence that our proposed phrase generation technique based on the symmetric extension of high-noise words as well as the inverse feature density term weighting function (both described in section2.2) provide better retrieval performance in a WAN information environment than any other retrieval method.
