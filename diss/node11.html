Preference, Relevance, and Communication

   
...it is important for responsible scientists, academicians, popularizers, and researchers to do all in their power to write articles and present papers that have the power to compel the reader to move from reading to doing. -- C. Bernier & A. Yerkey, Cogent Communication: Overcoming Reading Information Overload p. 207.

Scientific and many other kinds of literature are usually not considered for their persuasive  power; instead one views them as a vehicle for the presentation of data in a rigid, unemotional format. However, most scientists truly want to influence opinion  and action  by their presentations; they would be disappointed if their work failed to do so. In fact, we would claim that any and all recorded information, be it a short message or a textbook, is intended by the author to elicit some action by the reader. Action here can mean anything from a small change in attitude to an immediate, physical reaction. We include the effectiveness of a message in eliciting an action by its reader, or the cogency of that message as part of the reader's preference judgement. Instead of asking a reader to rank retrieved messages according to their ``relevance to a current information need,'' we also ask the reader to consider ``the effectiveness of a message in eliciting an action.''

This quality  of message's relevance depends not only upon the message itself, but also on the sender, the receiver, and the medium. We shall analyse each of these aspects in turn.

the source 

It is well established that the receiver evaluates a message and the source of the message at the same time [Bern79], [Schri89]. This interaction can serve either to increase the message's persuasiveness or to decrease it. The attitude of the receiver toward the source is evaluated by the perceived credibility and the perceived identification. Credibility  includes expertness , subject proficiency , qualification , competence , authoritativeness , trustworthiness , etc. Aristotle  took a somewhat broader view of credibility and included such factors as apparent motive , character , fairness , friendliness , and good  sense. More recent literature in rhetoric  [Gros90] reveals another dimension of credibility called dynamism . Dynamism is the liveliness  or forcefulness in persuasion . Several methods of formalizing and quantifying these factors have been presented in the cognitive  psychology literature [Podp89]. Since we feel that the only useful assessments for cogency  are from the receiver, we account for the message source only by the receiver's evaluation  of the source.

the message itself

The main burden of eliciting action from a reader lies in the message itself: what is said, how it is stated and how the elements of the message are arranged . Although the source itself can be a means of persuasion, electronic media can serve to minimize this effect [Quar90], [Hilt81]. Because there are no physical cues other than the text or other media of a message, it is easier to concentrate on the topic  of discussion rather than the person behind the message.

To be cogent, it is important that a message be relatively free of noise, irrelevancies , and meanemes . It has been shown repeatedly that the removal of meanemes not only saves reading time, but can also improve the reader's assimilation , remembering , learning , and integration  of the information in the message [Bern79], [Hilt85]. Bernier  and Yerkey  recommend the dissemination of terse  conclusions and very dense  summaries, while Hiltz  and Turoff  prefer to limit the author  in the length of individual messages to ensure that communicated messages be concise . Both sets of authors admit that some flexibility is necessary in message length. None of the authors recommends a method for this flexibility. We feel that length  limitations and filtering should be exercised by the reader; as we shall see in chapter 3, we have designed software models to facilitate the reader's access to denser  paragraphs of a message (surrogates) and to filter  out unwanted paragraphs.

There are other important factors in the message that affect its cogency and therefore a reader's preferences concerning it. A message's composition  or formulation  is significant. This dimension includes the methods and order of presentation , the expressiveness  of the arguments , and how well the message establishes the motivational  goals and values [Shar90]. Another prominent aspect of cogency and relative relevance is the message's appeal . It can appeal to the reader emotionally, logically , or syllogistically . Finally, there is the inherent credibility  of the message itself. We believe that the receiver's evaluation of these factors is the most important part of a the cogency aspect of a relevance measure.

the receiver

It is helpful both to a sender, and to the receiver himself to be cognizant of the general factors inherent in a receiver which affect message relevance. The so-called personal  characteristics of a receiver , such as self-image , open-mindedness , anxiety , intelligence , as well as demographic  characteristics such as age  and gender  are extremely variable, impossible to control [Bern79], and are not pertinent to our purpose. We concentrate on a more tangible and measurable trait, namely the concept of cognitive consistency  or rational evaluation  [Tobi89].

The receiver of a message maintains cognitive consistency during the reception process by ``fitting '' an incoming message into his existing attitude-belief      structure [McGr69]. The reception  process includes automatic filtering steps that minimize incongruities  between existing attitudes  and any new, plausible , convincing messages. When inconsistencies arise, the receiver may reject, ignore, misinterpret, distort, or change the message. Or, he may change his existing attitude to concur with the message. Individuals avoid situations which produce cognitive dissonance and inconsistencies, selectively exposing themselves to persuasive  appeals  with which they agree. Persuasion usually requires several different messages over time to change embedded attitudes . When strong and repeated messages at odds with a receiver's attitude persist, the receiver will go through stages of acceptance , starting with avoidance  and progressing through phases of rationalization , distortion , and attitude differentiation  [Bern79]. Our thesis is that by filtering messages more effectively, a receiver can act with better information (more informedly ) and therefore more confidently.

the medium 

There is strong evidence [Wyle87], [Goul84], [Rein85] that subtle but far-reaching differences in comprehension , retention , and relevance  result from differences in the medium  used for presenting a message. For many applications in which the data to be presented are text, paper  is measurably superior to glass  (a computer  display) for retention and comprehension [Wyle87]. Additionally, the demand for graphics  and multi-media in the computer marketplace indicates that text itself is not the only medium of choice for communicating most messages. Although we do not agree with all of his assertions, Marshal McLuhan   [Velt91] is probably correct in his contention that media  are, in some sense, an extension of individuals . The apperception of a message is greatly affected by the medium  in which it is expressed. We shall limit ourselves to computer-related media ; the advantages of so-called multi-media  in communicating a message are clear:

  • More expressive power: Media other than text give an author more and stronger channels of communication
  • Compactness: A picture can be worth a thousand words
  • Involvement of more senses: Multi-media includes sound, which can enhance the expressive power as well as the comprehension and retention of the message

Thus differences in the form of the communications medium itself can play a delicate but meaningful rôle in the persuasiveness, relevance, and therefore the preferences and impact  of a message .
