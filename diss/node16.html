Text Structure

         

IR research has an unfortunate lack of simple techniques which take into account the coarse structure  of messages in indexing and retrieval methods. Full-blown natural language processing (NLP)  is complex,    difficult, and computationally expensive [Maul91]. It can also unfortunately yield poor retrieval results. We therefore explore the use of statistical text analysis using paragraph structure.  

Text, by definition, is composed of strings of letters, delimiters, and punctuation .  Practically all messages, in any collection, contain additional structure of varying degree. The text contained in any message adheres to the grammatical and linguistical rules of the language in which it is written. Additionally, the distinct sentences in a text separate the ideas it contains. Paragraphs,   sections, and chapters add further hierarchical structure. Even the positions of words, phrases, and sentences in a paragraph or in a section are significant factors in the semantic meaning   of the text. As noted  earlier, we preclude the use of NLP parsing   for feature weighting based on message structure. Instead, we concentrate on a simpler, more rigid structure inherent in the WAN   messages themselves.

In these collections, certain paragraphs are designed to be semantically dense indices into the full-text of the message. These paragraphs are usually displayed to the reader before the full-text of a message. In fact, WAN message readers will often base their decision of whether to read or reject a message on these content-identifier paragraphs. Such paragraphs are called message headers. These     headers include subject, author, keywords, summary, references, and other formally defined fields [Hort87]. Most WAN message authors, because of the limited size and large importance of these fields, will exert a significant effort in trying to express the meaning   of their entire message in the small space afforded in these fields. It is then logical to hypothesize that giving the indexing features found in these paragraphs more weight in indexing and retrieval algorithms will give such algorithms significantly better retrieval performance than algorithms that weight such features the same as those found in the full-text paragraphs. We would therefore like to weight the component features of such paragraphs differently from the full-text of the rest of the message.
