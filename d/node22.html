Automatic Phrase Generation

       

Throughout the last three decades in the IR, computer science, and other literature, there have been repeated attempts to incorporate phrases, i.e. combinations of words, in the automatic indexing and retrieval processes for full text applications [Salt83], [Jac92a], [Crof91]. The improvements gained through the use of phrases have counter-intuitively always been quite small. In some cases they have produced no improvement to the retrieval process at all. In very small message collections, with narrow, well-defined domains of discourse and small vocabularies, NLP methods using linguistically parsed phrases produce significant retrieval performance improvements [Jaco92]. But for larger unrestricted collections, most simple algorithmic phrase production methods to produce indexing features composed of more than one word have not had much success.

      In [Crof91], Croft et. al. analyse the major phrase production and indexing methods reported in the IR literature, then go on to propose and test related, slightly better methods. Retrieval algorithms based on their methods produce at best an 8-15% performance improvement over methods they tested that use individual words. The most successful automatic phrase generation system they tested uses Ken Church's stochastic tagger [Chur88] which Croft et. al. employed only to generate noun phrases. The stochastic tagger is based on so-called ``shallow'' lexical analysis as well as probability tables of contextual occurrence to assign parts of speech to words and also to identify noun phrase boundaries. This application is an example of the maturity that the best NLP  methods have obtained, as we mentioned in section 1.4. Most methods reviewed and applied by Croft et. al. are based on a semantic analysis of text, where the homeosemy  between a query text and each message in a collection is enhanced through meaningful groups of words.

Our approach to the generation and use of indexing features composed of more than one word takes a radically different approach. Teufel  [Teuf89] showed conclusively that extending overlapping word fragments (N-grams) based on their high information theoretical noise  produced a much superior set of features (used as indexing tokens) than using a fixed-size set of N-grams. Intrigued by this idea and Teufel's positive results, we decided to extend certain words into phrases based on their information theoretic noise within individual dynamic message collections. Words with a high noise value relative to other words in these collections are extended symmetrically to phrases and phrases with high-noise are themselves extended to longer phrases. Phrase construction starts with the selection of single-word features from a query text or message using the usual methods of word extraction, stop-list word elimination, and word reduction as described in [Wyle91].

The noise of each single-word feature, the average noise, and the standard deviation are then calculated. Those words with a noise value larger than the average noise plus one standard deviation are extended to phrases. The number of phrases constructed per high-noise word depends on the collection frequency of the word and on a distance   parameter . Phrases are formed from all combinations of each occurrence of the high-noise word with words to the left and words to the right of the high-noise word. Here is the first significant difference between Teufel's N-gram extension scheme and our phrase construction analog. Whereas Teufel extended N-grams only to the right; we form phrases symmetrically. phrases are formed for each high-noise word. Obviously, a large will cause explosively many phrases to be formed, and a small may fail to form important phrases. In his automatic phrase construction experiments, Fagan [Faga87] found an optimal value to be either 1 or 2, depending on the collection, and Croft [Crof91] verified this result. Again, the best analysis of this parameter is due to Fagan [Faga87] who found the optimal value to be either 2 or 3. For our method, experience indicates that provides the best results.

As we shall see in our discussion of retrieval performance experiments, the use of phrases generated by this scheme produces significant retrieval performance improvements over the use of single-term features or phrases produced by existing phrase generation methods.

In this chapter we introduced the passive subscriber model which is based on the SDI and software agent concepts; the model includes two new methods of human - computer interaction: palettes of examples are used in conjunction with a dialog system in which each message a software system sends to a subscriber is actually a valid command which can be interpreted by the system. Furthermore, we presented a hybrid of the SDI concept that uses modern IR similarity measures and dynamic message collections. We also defined an information server in terms of subscriber profile parameters and individual filtering stages used to extract information from the Matrix. Some properties of filtering stages were discussed in general, and four stages designed specifically to produce better retrieval performance in an IS were defined in detail. Extensions of the commonly used formulation of regular expressions were introduced briefly. Finally, a new method of automatic phrase construction for generating indexing tokens was defined based on the noise measure, and a more efficient re-formulation of that measure was presented.
